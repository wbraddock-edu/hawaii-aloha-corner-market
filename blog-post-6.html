Community Markets on Molokai: A Local Guide

CommunityMarch 28, 2026 · By Hoku Pali

One of the things that makes Molokai uniquely special is its abundance of informal food networks — roadside stands, community markets, church sales, and backyard growers who sell direct. These informal markets are woven into island life, and navigating them is both a practical skill and a joyful part of being a Molokai resident. Here's your guide to finding fresh food across the island.

Kaunakakai Town Markets

Kaunakakai is the commercial hub of Molokai, and Ala Malama Avenue — our main street — hosts several options for fresh local food. In addition to Aloha Corner Market, you'll find small vendors who set up tables on weekends offering produce, baked goods, and prepared foods. These informal weekend markets typically run from early morning until goods sell out, so arriving by 8 AM is recommended.

The Molokai Farmers Market operates periodically near the harbor area. Timing and vendors vary — follow local community boards and social media for current schedules. You'll typically find taro, sweet potatoes, tropical fruits, vegetables, fresh fish, and sometimes prepared foods like poke and plate lunches.

Roadside Stands

Some of the best produce on Molokai is sold from informal roadside stands operated by farmers and their families. These stands appear and disappear with the seasons — you might find a table piled with avocados one week and mangoes the next. The honor system is common: a price list, a box for payment, and fresh produce for whoever comes by. This system runs on community trust, and it works beautifully.

Look for stands along Kamehameha V Highway between Kaunakakai and the east end of the island, and along Maunaloa Highway heading west. East Molokai's rainier climate produces excellent tropical fruits, while the west and central parts of the island are better for sweet potatoes and other root vegetables.

Church and Community Sales

Molokai's churches are integral to community life, and many hold regular food sales — plate lunches, baked goods, and sometimes produce from church members' farms. These events also serve as informal community gatherings where you'll find the full social fabric of island life on display. Check local bulletin boards and the Molokai Dispatch for upcoming events.

Connecting with Farmers Directly

One of the best ways to ensure a consistent supply of fresh local food is to build a direct relationship with a farmer. Many Molokai farmers are open to regular customers picking up weekly boxes of produce, or can accommodate special requests for quantities they don't typically bring to market. Ask around — in a community of 7,000, connections are just a conversation away.

At Aloha Corner Market, we can often connect customers with our supplier farmers for larger quantities or specialty items. If you're looking for bulk taro for a family celebration, specific varieties of sweet potato, or large quantities of fresh fish, give us a call — we're happy to help you find what you need.
