Shopping Local vs. Big Box Stores in Hawaii

LifestyleMay 8, 2026 · By Malia Kahananui

Hawaii is one of the most food-import-dependent states in the nation. Over 85% of the food consumed in Hawaii is shipped in from the mainland. That dependency creates fragility — as we saw during supply chain disruptions in 2020 and 2021 when store shelves emptied within days. It also has a direct economic cost: every dollar spent at a chain grocery store leaves Hawaii almost entirely, while local stores recirculate that money within the community.

The Price Question

Let's address the elephant in the room: yes, some items may cost slightly more at a small local market than at a large chain. But the gap is much smaller than people assume, especially when you're comparing like for like. Our Molokai sweet potatoes, for instance, are cheaper than imported varieties at chain stores because there's no shipping cost. Our fresh fish is fresher and often less expensive than chain store options because it travels miles instead of thousands of miles.

When you factor in travel time and fuel cost to reach a big box store (often off-island for Molokai residents), the economics shift further in favor of local. A trip to Maui to shop at a chain store can cost $150–300 round trip in ferry or flight costs alone, eliminating any savings.

Quality and Freshness

Freshness is where local markets win decisively. Our produce was typically harvested within the last 1–3 days. Chain store produce has often traveled 5–14 days before hitting the shelf. That difference is visible and tasteful — locally grown tomatoes actually taste like tomatoes. Papaya ripened on the tree is incomparably better than papaya gassed into ripeness in a shipping container.

Product Selection

Chain stores excel at breadth of packaged goods — imported brands, national products, processed foods. Local markets excel at depth in local, fresh, and culturally specific products. We carry things no chain store would bother with: specific varieties of taro for poi-making, fresh breadfruit, Molokai honey, locally made mochi, Hawaiian sea salts.

For families maintaining Hawaiian food traditions, local markets are essential. The foods that define our culture simply aren't reliably available at chain stores. That's not a small thing — food is identity, and local markets preserve both.

Community and Relationships

When you shop at Aloha Corner Market, the person behind the counter knows your name. They remember that you like your fish cut a specific way. They'll set aside the last breadfruit for you when you call ahead. That relationship-based commerce is the bedrock of small community life and something no chain store algorithm can replicate. Shopping local keeps Molokai human.
