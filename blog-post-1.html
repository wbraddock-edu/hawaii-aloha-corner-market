The Complete Guide to Molokai's Local Produce

Local Farming June 1, 2026 · By Malia Kahananui 8 min read

Molokai is one of Hawaii's most agricultural islands — a place where the land is still worked the old way, with patience, respect, and deep knowledge passed down through generations. At Aloha Corner Market, we're lucky enough to source directly from the farmers and families who grow this remarkable food. In this guide, we'll walk you through the most important local crops you'll find at our store throughout the year.

Molokai Sweet Potato — The Island's Crown Jewel

Nothing says Molokai quite like the Molokai sweet potato — a deep purple-fleshed variety known worldwide for its nutty, rich flavor and striking color. Unlike the orange varieties you'll find at mainland grocery chains, the Molokai sweet potato (often called the Okinawan sweet potato locally) is dense, subtly sweet, and packed with antioxidants that give it that vivid purple interior.

These are grown on the island's fertile central plateau, where the red volcanic soil and cool overnight temperatures create ideal growing conditions. They're available year-round, with peak harvests in fall and early winter. You can roast them, steam them, mash them into a gorgeous violet purée, or use them in haupia (coconut pudding) for a stunning dessert.

Taro — Ancient Staple, Living Tradition

Taro is the most culturally significant food in all of Hawaii. In Native Hawaiian tradition, taro (kalo) is regarded as an elder sibling of the Hawaiian people, and it has been cultivated on these islands for over a thousand years. Molokai's taro patches are among the most beautiful in the state — flooded paddies called lo'i that reflect the sky and nurture these incredible plants.

At the market, we carry both raw taro root and prepared poi — the fermented taro paste that has been a Hawaiian dietary staple since ancient times. Poi is an acquired taste for newcomers but beloved by locals. It's naturally gluten-free, easily digestible, and extremely nutritious. Fresh taro can also be used in soups, stews, chips, and even desserts.

Breadfruit — The Tree of Abundance

'Ulu (breadfruit) is one of the most impressive plants in the Hawaiian landscape — a massive tree that produces enormous starchy fruits that can feed a family. When unripe, breadfruit is used like a potato — roasted, fried, or steamed. When ripe, it develops a soft, sweet, custard-like interior perfect for baking.

Molokai has a breadfruit restoration movement underway, with community organizations planting ulu trees throughout the island. We source our breadfruit from several local farms and occasionally from backyard trees whose owners bring us surplus fruit. It's nutritious, versatile, and truly island.

Tropical Fruits: Mango, Papaya & Banana

Molokai's warm, rainy climate is perfect for tropical fruits. Our papaya comes in several local varieties including the small, sweet Strawberry papaya. Mangoes arrive in summer — watch for Hayden, Rapoza, and Filipino varieties, each with a distinct flavor profile. Apple bananas are the local favorite — shorter, thicker, and much sweeter than the Cavendish bananas at mainland stores.

Buying seasonal means you're getting fruit at its peak — picked ripe from trees that were grown with care rather than shipping-optimized industrial farming. Stop by on Tuesdays and Fridays when our produce deliveries arrive for the freshest selection.

Supporting the Farmers Behind Your Food

Every locally sourced item at Aloha Corner Market comes with a story — a family, a farm, a tradition. When you choose local produce over imported alternatives, you're keeping money in the Molokai economy, reducing carbon footprints from shipping, and honoring the agricultural heritage of this remarkable island. We're proud to be the bridge between our amazing local growers and your family's table.

Ask any of our team members about what's freshest that day — we always know exactly where our produce came from and who grew it. That's the Aloha Corner Market difference.
