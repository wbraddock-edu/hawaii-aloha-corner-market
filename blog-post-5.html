The Hawaiian Superfoods Guide

NutritionApril 10, 2026 · By Malia Kahananui

Long before the term "superfood" was coined by modern nutritionists, Hawaiians were cultivating and eating some of the most nutritionally dense plants on the planet. Traditional Hawaiian diet — centered on taro, fish, sweet potato, breadfruit, and sea vegetables — supported one of the healthiest populations in Polynesia. Let's explore the standout superfoods available right here in our market.

Taro (Kalo) — The Sacred Superfood

Taro is nutritionally remarkable. It's an excellent source of dietary fiber, manganese, potassium, and vitamins B6 and E. It has a lower glycemic index than many other starches, making it suitable for people managing blood sugar levels. As poi — fermented taro paste — it also contains beneficial probiotics that support gut health. Poi is one of the most digestible foods known, historically used as a first food for Hawaiian babies.

Molokai Sweet Potato (Okinawan Purple)

The deep purple flesh of Molokai's signature sweet potato is loaded with anthocyanins — the same antioxidant compounds found in blueberries and acai. Anthocyanins have been linked to reduced inflammation, improved cardiovascular health, and neuroprotective effects. They're also rich in fiber, vitamins A and C, and potassium. Nutritionally, they outperform regular sweet potatoes significantly.

Limu (Hawaiian Sea Vegetables)

Limu is the Hawaiian term for seaweed, and it encompasses dozens of edible varieties that grow along Hawaii's coastlines. Traditional Hawaiians ate limu daily — in salads, as garnishes on fish, or eaten fresh from the ocean. Modern research has confirmed what Hawaiian tradition already knew: seaweed is extraordinarily nutritious, rich in iodine, omega-3 fatty acids, vitamins, and minerals. Several varieties have demonstrated anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties in laboratory studies.

Noni (Indian Mulberry)

Noni is perhaps the most powerful and polarizing of Hawaiian superfoods. The fruit, which smells pungent when ripe, has been used medicinally in Hawaii for centuries to treat skin conditions, inflammation, digestive issues, and pain. Modern studies have found noni juice contains antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and possibly immunostimulating compounds. It's an acquired taste, but traditional Hawaiian healers considered it one of the most versatile medicinal plants available.

Breadfruit ('Ulu)

Breadfruit is calorie-dense and rich in complex carbohydrates, making it an excellent energy food for active lifestyles. It also contains significant fiber, potassium, and vitamin C. Research has shown that breadfruit may have hypoglycemic effects, making it potentially beneficial for type 2 diabetes management. The University of Hawaii has been studying breadfruit extensively as both a food security crop and a functional food.
