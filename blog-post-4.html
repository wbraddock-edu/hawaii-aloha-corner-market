Molokai's Rich Agricultural History

HistoryApril 25, 2026 · By Lani Akana

Molokai is known as the most Hawaiian of the Hawaiian Islands — a place where ancient traditions, subsistence farming, and deep cultural ties to the land persist more strongly than anywhere else in the state. The island's agricultural history stretches back over a thousand years, long before Western contact, and understanding it helps us appreciate why what grows here is so special.

Ancient Hawaiian Agriculture

Pre-contact Hawaiians on Molokai were sophisticated agriculturalists who developed intricate systems for growing food in diverse conditions. The island's north coast was home to elaborate taro lo'i (flooded paddies) fed by streams from the towering sea cliffs — some of the tallest in the world. These lo'i produced enormous quantities of taro, which was pounded into poi and formed the dietary cornerstone of the Hawaiian diet.

On the drier south coast, Hawaiians constructed fishponds — massive stone-walled enclosures that trapped fish at high tide and allowed them to grow to harvest size. Molokai once had over 60 fishponds along its southern shoreline, producing mullet, milkfish, and other species year-round. These were engineering marvels, built without modern tools by communities working in concert over generations.

The Plantation Era

In the 19th and early 20th centuries, Molokai's agricultural landscape shifted dramatically with the arrival of plantation agriculture. Large portions of the island were given over to pineapple and sugar cultivation by Dole and other companies. For decades, the plantation economy defined Molokai's economic life, bringing in workers from Japan, the Philippines, Portugal, and elsewhere who would become part of the island's multicultural fabric.

The plantation era ended in the mid-20th century as industrial agriculture consolidated elsewhere and costs made Hawaiian plantation farming uneconomical. Dole's pineapple operations ceased in 1975, leaving behind a changed landscape but also an opportunity for diversified agriculture to re-emerge.

Modern Agricultural Revival

Today, Molokai is home to a mix of ranching (Molokai Ranch remains one of the largest landowners), small organic farms, and a revival of traditional Hawaiian agriculture. Organizations like the Hui o Kuapā have worked to restore ancient fishponds. Taro cultivation is being revived by families reconnecting with their ancestral practices. Small farms growing sweet potatoes, vegetables, tropical fruits, and herbs are flourishing.
