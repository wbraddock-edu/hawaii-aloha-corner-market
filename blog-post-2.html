Why Supporting Small Island Businesses Matters

Community May 20, 2026 · By Keoni Kahananui 7 min read

Molokai is a small island — about 38 miles long and 10 miles wide — with a population of roughly 7,000 people. There are no traffic lights, no big box stores, and no national fast food chains on our island. That's not an accident. It's a choice that the Molokai community has made, consciously and repeatedly, to preserve the character, pace, and independence that makes this island so extraordinary.

The Multiplier Effect of Local Spending

When you buy a bag of sweet potatoes from Aloha Corner Market instead of ordering from an online retailer, something remarkable happens. That dollar doesn't leave Molokai — it cycles through the local economy in ways that compound. We pay our local employees, who spend money at other local businesses. We pay our local suppliers, who in turn support their own families and vendors. Economists call this the "local multiplier effect," and studies show that every dollar spent at a locally owned business generates two to four times more local economic activity than the same dollar spent at a chain store.

For a small island like Molokai, where the economic ecosystem is especially fragile, this multiplier is critical. There are no corporate subsidies, no national buying power to fall back on. It's just us, our community, and the choices we make every day.

Jobs, Dignity, and Opportunity

Small businesses are Molokai's largest employers. We hire local teenagers for their first jobs. We give longtime residents stable employment that lets them stay on the island rather than relocating to Honolulu or the mainland for work. Chain stores, when they do come to island communities, often displace locally owned alternatives while paying lower wages and extracting profits off-island.

At Aloha Corner Market, every employee is a neighbor. Many of our team members have worked here for over a decade. We offer flexible schedules for families, accommodate school events, and invest in our employees' growth. That kind of stability and dignity simply doesn't exist when the business model is purely extractive.

Preserving Cultural Identity

Small businesses are the custodians of cultural memory. When you walk into Aloha Corner Market, you find poi, limu kohu, Molokai sweet potatoes, and fresh ahi — foods that have been part of Hawaiian life for centuries. National grocery chains carry standardized product lines optimized for national consumer trends. They don't know what grows on Molokai. They don't carry the things that make Molokai food culture distinct.

Supporting local businesses means ensuring that the recipes, flavors, and traditions of your community survive. It means the next generation of Molokai kids can grow up knowing what fresh breadfruit tastes like, where to find the best poi, and why Molokai honey is unlike anything else in the world.

Environmental Responsibility

Shipping goods to and across Hawaii has an enormous carbon footprint. Everything that doesn't grow here must be flown or shipped in. When you choose locally grown produce over imported alternatives, you're reducing the environmental cost of your food in a meaningful way. Local supply chains are shorter, simpler, and substantially more sustainable. Molokai's agricultural practices — many of which are organic or low-input by default — also contribute to a healthier local ecosystem.

What You Can Do

The most powerful thing any Molokai resident can do for the local economy is simply to choose local first. Before ordering online, check what's available at your neighborhood stores. Before choosing the cheapest option, consider the full cost — economic, cultural, environmental — of where that money goes. Every purchase is a vote for the kind of community you want to live in.

We're grateful for every customer who walks through our door, not just because it keeps our lights on, but because it's a statement of values. Thank you for choosing Molokai.
